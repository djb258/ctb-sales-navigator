## The Foundation — The 90/15 Rule

Any group splits into two populations:

- **90% of employees cause only 15% of claims/cost.** Healthy population. Routine stuff. They need a simple plan — no copays, no deductibles, just a card that works. Day-to-day transactions. We're not managing this group.
- **10% of employees cause 85% of claims/cost.** This is where the money is. Cancer, surgeries, high-dollar drugs, chronic conditions. THIS is what we manage. This is where every dollar is won or lost.

The 90% get one plan — simple, low-admin. The 10% get the concierge, the routing, the vendor network, the active management.

**One-liner:** "You can't stop claims. Claims are going to happen no matter what. The trick is to manage them, not overpay."

**One-liner:** "Health insurance is nothing more than a bill-paying system. We have to manage the cost."

## The Concierge Model — Managing the 10%

When something big happens (the 10%), the concierge reaches out to the employee — hand-holding, human touch, "we're going to help you through this."

The concierge is a **router/orchestrator**. Many-to-one relationship — multiple vendors on the back side, but the employee only ever deals with one person.

**Vendors never talk to employees directly.** The concierge warms it up, figures out which vendor needs to handle it, and routes.

### Hospital Claims (Pre-cert — BEFORE the event)

Pre-certification is the trigger. Before the employee goes to the hospital, you know what's coming. Route BEFORE the bill exists.

Three pricing paths for every hospital in a 30-50 mile radius of the prospect's zip code:

1. **PPO Network** — in-network negotiated rates. Standard path.
2. **Reference-Based Pricing (RBP)** — pay based on a reference rate (% of Medicare). Better than PPO.
3. **501R** — nonprofit hospitals required to offer financial assistance. Best case — can go to zero.

The concierge checks the hospital map, picks the cheapest path, pushes to that vendor.

**Example:** $100,000 hospital bill → 501R → $0. Both employer and employee benefit.

**Research per prospect:** Pull every hospital within 30-50 mile radius of their zip code. Flag which are 501(c)(3) nonprofits (501R eligible), which accept RBP, which are PPO in-network. This data feeds Gate 2 video.

### The Concierge Introduction Process (100% engagement rate)

The concierge / medical shopper is the human layer in front of the vendor routing (PDF 03). The employee never navigates the vendor network — they just know someone helped them.

**Two-step warm intro:**
1. **Email from HR** — looks like it comes from the employee's own HR department, not a random vendor. "We have a service that helps with your situation." Trusted source. Employee expects what's coming.
2. **Concierge calls** — employee is already warmed up. Concierge builds the relationship, understands the situation, then routes to the best-priced vendor.

**Result: 100% engagement rate.** Compare that to a vendor calling an employee cold out of the blue — they don't answer, don't trust it, think it's a scam. Dave's way: HR email (familiar) → concierge (personal) → vendor handoff (warm). Three layers of trust before a vendor ever touches the employee.

The HR email is automated outreach — SID/MID territory. Template is the constant, employee name and situation is the variable.

### Drug Claims (AFTER the claim happens)

High-dollar drug shows up in claims → concierge identifies it → reaches out to employee → routes to cheapest source:

1. **Manufacturer Assistance Programs (MAPs)** — get it from the manufacturer directly
2. **International** — source it from overseas
3. **340B** — federal discount program pricing

Once identified, the drug is managed on every refill going forward. Not a one-time save — recurring monthly savings.

## Stop-Loss Education Chain

Prospect needs to understand how the money works before seeing their numbers:

1. **Fully Insured** — you pay premiums, insurance company keeps the surplus. You overpay, they win. No transparency, no upside.
2. **Level Funded** — fixed monthly payment with a claims fund underneath. Some surplus return, but limited. Training wheels.
3. **Traditional Stop-Loss (Spec + Agg)**
   - **Specific (spec)** — caps exposure on any single employee's claims
   - **Aggregate (agg)** — caps total group exposure across all employees
   - Self-insured but protected from catastrophic hits
4. **Captive Insurance** — the big play. Pool with other groups. **If you don't spend the premiums, you get them back.** Direct financial incentive to manage claims well.

**The thesis:** Captive + concierge managing the 10% = you never hit your stop-loss = premiums come back. The concierge isn't just a nice service — it's the engine that drives the financial outcome.

## Monte Carlo Simulation — The Divergence Story

**Year 0 (historical):**
- You spent **X** (what you actually paid, no programs)
- With our programs, you would have spent **Y** (40-50% less)

**The mistake everyone makes:**
- People think the savings are flat (X - Y every year)
- Wrong. The baseline keeps climbing.
- Path A (do nothing): costs compound on top of inflated X
- Path B (implement): costs compound on top of reduced Y
- **The gap widens every year.** Compounding off a lower base.

**Key framing:** "If you had our programs in place, you spent X. You would have spent Y. In the following years, if you spent Y with our programs, you would have spent X not doing anything."

Personalized to THEIR numbers, THEIR claims history, THEIR employee count. Not generic.

## Cost Split

It's always a combination of employer and employee paying:

- Employee only
- Employee + spouse
- Employee + child/children
- Family

Each tier has employer's share and employee's share. When you reduce overall cost through managing claims, both sides benefit. Retention and recruitment tool — "we put money back in your employees' pockets."

## The Three Silos

1. **Product** — the actual insurance (stop-loss, dental, life, vision, etc.). **Zero commission.** No incentive to push higher premiums. You quote all carriers, present options, employer picks whoever they want. You don't care.

2. **Operations** — Dave's silo. The platform: concierge, routing, dashboards, Monte Carlo, claims management, video pipeline. The engine. **Paid PEPM.**

3. **Service** — boots on the ground with employees. Three options by budget:
   - **HR department** does it themselves (cheapest)
   - **AFLAC agent** handles it (middle)
   - **Another advisor/benefits person** (most expensive)
   Client picks. **Paid PEPM.**

Stripping commissions out of Product = full transparency. PEPM on Operations and Service = client knows exactly what they're paying. Zero incentive to inflate anything.

## Renewal

If you're doing your job all year — managing the 10%, routing claims, keeping costs under stop-loss — renewal is a non-event. Captive returns surplus. No fire drill, no scrambling for new carriers.

Renewal conversation: "Here's what we spent. Here's what we saved. Here's your money back."

Traditional broker earns their fee at renewal (scrambling). You earn your fee all year (managing). Renewal is the scorecard.

## Client Experience — 5 Dashboards + Employee Web Page

### Five Dashboards (management-facing)

1. **Underwriting** — claims data, risk profile, stop-loss positioning
2. **Renewal** — where you stand heading into renewal, no surprises
3. **HR** — employee management, enrollment, ticket status visibility
4. **CEO/CFO** — cost, savings, captive returns, ROI
5. **Advisor** — Dave's cockpit, manages the whole account

### Employee Web Page (employee-facing)

Each client gets their own web page with:
- All plan information — what's covered, how it works
- **Ticketing system** — employee submits ticket, auto-routes to correct vendor based on benefit type

### The Service Loop (Closed-Loop System)

1. Employee submits ticket
2. System routes to correct vendor automatically
3. Vendor handles the issue
4. Service person reaches out to employee — "Was this resolved?"
5. Ticket closed

**Not closed until the service person has talked to the employee.** The service person's dashboard is their work queue — open tickets needing close-out calls. Focused on the 10% (high-dollar claims).

HR sees ticket status on their dashboard too. Full visibility without being the middleman.

**HR becomes the auditor, not the operator.** They watch the dashboard, confirm the system is working, step in only when something breaks. The automation takes work off HR's plate — they go from being the router to being oversight.

## The Transition — Census Enrollment

The biggest fear: "Switching sounds like a nightmare."

The answer: **Census enrollment. Take the people already on the plan and copy them over. Done.**

- No re-enrollment
- No employees picking new plans
- No disruption
- Same people, same coverage, different engine underneath
- Employees barely notice except the card looks different

Gate 3 point: Kill the transition fear in one sentence. HR is worried about the workload. CEO is worried about employee complaints. This eliminates both.

## The Moat — Why the Model is Unpoachable

**Real story:** A competing broker tried to do a broker of record (BOR) change on one of Dave's clients. They didn't know what to do because there was no commission. They had to go to the client and ask to get paid for something the client was already getting as part of the PEPM package.

**Why it works:**
- BOR only swaps the product (carrier). Dave isn't on the product side — he's operations.
- To remove operations, you have to rip out the entire system: dashboards, employee web page, ticketing, concierge routing, vendor network, Monte Carlo, claims management.
- A competing broker can swap a carrier in an afternoon. They can't rebuild the platform.
- **Dave doesn't care about BORs.** "I'm operations. To take me out, you have to take out the whole system."

**Continuous vendor optimization:** Dave is always evaluating new vendors to cut costs. The vendor lineup isn't static — if a better 501R vendor, a cheaper international pharmacy source, or a new MAP program appears, it gets swapped in. The client sees costs keep going down. The concierge keeps routing. The employee sees nothing change. That's the PEPM justification: "You're paying me to continuously find ways to reduce your cost. Every better deal I find flows to your bottom line."

**The deepening effect:**
- Year 1: valuable
- Year 3: embedded (data, history, optimized routing, proven captive returns)
- Year 5: you ARE the operating system
- Every year deeper = harder to replace. Not because of a contract — because of accumulated value.

**Commission brokers are replaceable** — they all sell the same carriers. Operations is not replaceable. The engine gets more valuable over time.

**Video point (Gate 3 or Gate 1):** "Our model protects you from broker churn. Nobody can walk in and take your account because there's nothing for them to take. The value is in the platform, not the commission."

**The dare:** Dave actively encourages clients to compete the product. "Take it out to every broker. Get their best deal. Their quote has commission baked into the premium. Mine doesn't. Same carrier, same plan — yours is cheaper because there's zero commission." The competing broker cannot match the price on the same product because they need to get paid through the premium. Even if they get close on product, they don't have the platform. They're offering a carrier swap. Dave's offering an operating system.

**Gate 4 point:** "I encourage you to compete it. That's how you'll know this works."

## The Competitive Position — Why Nobody Can Replicate This

To replicate this model you have to be all four things at once:

1. **Next to the client** — advisor relationship, face to face, trust. A TPA can't do this — they're back-office, processing claims, no CEO relationship.
2. **Building the platform** — operations, dashboards, routing, automation, ticketing. A traditional broker can't do this — they're on spreadsheets and email.
3. **Managing the vendors** — concierge, pre-cert routing, drug management, continuous vendor evaluation. A tech company can't do this — they don't know 501R from RBP and they've never managed a captive.
4. **Not on commission** — aligned incentives, fully transparent. Most brokers can't do this — their business model depends on carrier commissions.

Miss any one of those four and the model falls apart:
- **TPA** could try operations but they're not the advisor — no client relationship, no product control
- **Tech company** could build the platform but they don't understand insurance domain
- **Another broker** could try but they're on commission and don't have the tech stack
- **Sales navigator/consultant** might have the relationship but no platform, no vendor network

The moat is the intersection: advisor + operator + technologist + domain expert, sitting right next to the client with zero financial conflicts. That position doesn't exist anywhere else in the food chain.
